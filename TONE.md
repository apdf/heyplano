# HeyPlano — Brand Voice & Tone
**Version:** 1.0
**Date:** April 2026

---

## What HeyPlano sounds like

A knowledgeable friend who bought property in Spain, found it genuinely maddening, figured it out anyway, and now wants to make sure you don't go through the same thing blind.

Not a startup. Not an agency. Not a lawyer. A person.

---

## The emotional job

Every piece of HeyPlano communication should make the reader feel one of three things, in this order of priority:

1. **Seen.** "They get it. This is exactly what I'm experiencing."
2. **Less alone.** "I'm not stupid. This process really is broken."
3. **In control.** "I know what to do next."

Never make them feel lectured. Never make them feel sold to.

---

## Voice principles

**Direct.** Say the thing. No preamble, no throat-clearing. If a sentence doesn't add something, cut it.

**Specific.** Generic claims destroy trust. "The process takes longer than you think" is weak. "The bank alone takes 6-10 weeks minimum, before the notary sees a single document" is useful.

**Warm but not soft.** We care about the reader. We're also slightly angry on their behalf. Both things are true at once.

**Honest about limits.** We don't know everything. We say so when we don't. That's part of what makes us trustworthy.

**Never corporate.** Banned words: seamless, leverage, synergy, empower, journey, solution, ecosystem, holistic, streamlined.

---

## Perspective

First person plural: **we**.

We went through this. We built this because of it. We're in it with you.

Not "HeyPlano offers..." — that's a press release.
"We learned the hard way that..." — that's a friend.

---

## What we are not

- Not an agent. Not a lawyer. Not a broker.
- Not neutral. We are structurally on the buyer's side.
- Not polished. Polished means someone paid for it.
- Not cheerful. The process is genuinely hard. We don't pretend otherwise.

---

## Practical rules

- Short sentences. Long ones when rhythm demands it, not by default.
- No exclamation marks. Ever.
- No emoji in formal content. Fine in conversational/email contexts if it fits naturally.
- Numbers over words: "6-10 weeks" not "several weeks."
- When in doubt, cut the last sentence. It's usually the one that over-explains.

---

## The one-sentence test

Before publishing anything, ask: **could this have been written by an agent trying to sound independent?**

If yes, rewrite it.
