A purchase that should be the best decision of their life,

"

A Norwegian family pays €2.1M for a villa above Marbella, primarily for the sea view. Their five intermediaries — agent, lawyer, mortgage broker, notary, surveyor — process the deal in three months.

Six months after closing, construction begins on an eight-story development directly in front of them. The urban planning permit was filed eighteen months earlier and sat publicly available at the local urbanism office. Nobody walked in.

The view they paid €600K extra for is now a wall.

A composite. Variations of this story repeat every month along this coast.

THE OPPORTUNITY
04 / 13

The flow is large,
growing, and underserved.

Cross-border residential purchasing is no longer a fringe of the market. It is one of the fastest-growing segments in European real estate, driven by remote work, climate migration, generational wealth transfer, and political flight from the United States.

Every transaction is high-value, high-margin, and structurally inefficient. A buyer who would never pay for advice in her home country pays a 5% kickback she cannot see, every time.

Spain is our entry market. The same engine works in Portugal, Italy, France, Greece, and beyond. Wherever a person buys property in a country they don't live in, the problem is the same.

The Family in Transition

"We need to know if our kids' school works before we love this house."

Dutch, German, Swedish, Belgian. €600K–€1.2M. Buying for lifestyle, family, partial relocation. Decision cycle is long. They want to be seen, not sold to.

"We want a future our home country no longer offers."

Coastal liberals, dual-citizenship hopefuls, Latam-tied. €800K–€2.5M. Driven by political and cultural exit. New segment, fast-growing, underserved by every existing channel.

European wealth, often older. €1.5M–€5M+. Has bought before and learned not to trust. Wants depth, not enthusiasm. Will not engage a sales channel. Will engage with someone structurally on her side.
